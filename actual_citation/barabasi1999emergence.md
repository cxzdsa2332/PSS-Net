# barabasi1999emergence

- **Title:** Emergence of scaling in random networks
- **Authors:** Albert-László Barabási, Réka Albert
- **Journal/Year:** Science, 286(5439), 509–512, 1999
- **DOI:** 10.1126/science.286.5439.509
- **Source consulted:** Semantic Scholar (DOI:10.1126/science.286.5439.509)

## Verbatim abstract

"Systems as diverse as genetic networks or the World Wide Web are best described as
networks with complex topology. A common property of many large networks is that the
vertex connectivities follow a scale-free power-law distribution. This feature was found
to be a consequence of two generic mechanisms: (i) networks expand continuously by the
addition of new vertices, and (ii) new vertices attach preferentially to sites that are
already well connected. A model based on these two ingredients reproduces the observed
stationary scale-free distributions, which indicates that the development of large
networks is governed by robust self-organizing phenomena that go beyond the particulars
of the individual systems."

## Supports in intro

B2 — high-impact empirical basis for the sparsity prior used in network inference: real
networks (genetic networks included) have scale-free, power-law vertex connectivity, so
most components are directly linked to only a few others. This justifies imposing
sparsity as the regularizer that turns interaction-function estimation into network
recovery. (Cited for the sparse/heavy-tailed connectivity claim, not for the
preferential-attachment growth model itself.)
