\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

We consider the problem of learning a directed coupling network from perturbed
steady-state observations of a nonlinear dynamical system.  The method is
designed for settings in which repeated interventions can be applied, the system
can be allowed to relax, and the resulting equilibrium state can be measured
more reliably than time derivatives.  The statistical object of interest is not
the full transient trajectory, but the collection of univariate steady-state
effects that explain how perturbations propagate through the system.

\subsection{A sparse additive steady-state model}
\label{sec:method:model}

Let $\xv(t)=(x_1(t),\ldots,x_p(t))^\top\in\R_+^p$ denote the state of $p$
interacting variables.  We use the additive ODE
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d x_j(t)}{dt}
  =
  \mu_j
  + f_{jj}\{x_j(t)\}
  + \sum_{i\neq j} f_{ji}\{x_i(t)\}
  + u_j(t),
  \qquad j=1,\ldots,p,
  \label{eq:ode}
\end{equation}
as the working model for perturbed steady states.  Here $\mu_j$ is a baseline
drift term, $u_j(t)$ is an externally controlled perturbation, $f_{jj}$ is the
self-feedback function for node $j$, and $f_{ji}$ for $i\neq j$ is the effect of
source node $i$ on target node $j$.

The directed network is defined by nonzero cross-node effects,
\begin{equation}
  E = \{(j\leftarrow i): f_{ji}\not\equiv 0,\ i\neq j\}.
  \label{eq:edge}
\end{equation}
Self-feedback functions are estimated because they affect equilibrium and local
stability, but they are not counted as directed network edges.

The decomposition in \eqref{eq:ode} has the usual additive identifiability issue:
a constant can be moved between $\mu_j$ and any $f_{ji}$.  We impose the
normalization
\begin{equation}
  f_{ji}(0)=0,\qquad i,j=1,\ldots,p,
  \label{eq:zero}
\end{equation}
so that all constant offsets are absorbed by $\mu_j$.  This convention also
matches the interpretation that a source variable at the zero reference level
contributes no additional effect.

\subsection{Perturbed steady-state observations}
\label{sec:method:pss}

Suppose that $N$ perturbation conditions
$\uv^{(1)},\ldots,\uv^{(N)}\in\R^p$ are applied.  For each condition, the system
is allowed to reach a stable equilibrium $\xv^{(k)*}$, and we observe
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{\xv}^{(k)} = \xv^{(k)*} + \bm{\eta}^{(k)},
  \qquad k=1,\ldots,N,
  \label{eq:obs}
\end{equation}
where $\bm{\eta}^{(k)}$ represents measurement noise.  At equilibrium,
\eqref{eq:ode} implies the algebraic constraint
\begin{equation}
  \mu_j
  + f_{jj}\{x_j^{(k)*}\}
  + \sum_{i\neq j} f_{ji}\{x_i^{(k)*}\}
  + u_j^{(k)}
  = 0,
  \qquad k=1,\ldots,N.
  \label{eq:ss}
\end{equation}
Thus, for each target node $j$, the negative perturbation $-u_j^{(k)}$ acts as
the response and the measured steady-state variables provide the predictors.
This is the key statistical reduction: estimation can proceed by regression
without numerical differentiation of noisy trajectories
\citep{meister2013pss,henderson2014network,wu2014saode}.

\subsection{Basis expansion}
\label{sec:method:basis}

We approximate each unknown univariate effect by a finite dictionary
$\psiv(a)=(\psi_1(a),\ldots,\psi_M(a))^\top$,
\begin{equation}
  f_{ji}(a)
  \approx
  \psiv(a)^\top \thetav_{ji}
  =
  \sum_{m=1}^{M} \theta_{jim}\psi_m(a),
  \qquad
  \thetav_{ji}\in\R^M.
  \label{eq:basis}
\end{equation}
The implementation uses the no-intercept monomial dictionary
$\psi_m(a)=a^m$, $m=1,\ldots,M$, which automatically satisfies
\eqref{eq:zero}.  Other no-intercept dictionaries can be substituted, but the
monomial dictionary has a useful interpretability property: a linear effect is
represented by a single nonzero coefficient, rather than being spread across
several basis functions.

For target $j$, define the stacked coefficient vector
\[
  \thetav_j
  =
  (\thetav_{j1}^\top,\ldots,\thetav_{jp}^\top)^\top
  \in \R^{pM},
\]
and construct the design matrix $\Psib\in\R^{N\times pM}$ with entries
\begin{equation}
  \Psib_{k,(i-1)M+m}
  =
  \psi_m\!\left(\tilde{x}_i^{(k)}\right),
  \qquad
  k=1,\ldots,N,\ i=1,\ldots,p,\ m=1,\ldots,M.
  \label{eq:design}
\end{equation}
Stacking \eqref{eq:ss} over the $N$ perturbation conditions gives the node-wise
linear working model
\begin{equation}
  \Psib\thetav_j + \mu_j\mathbf{1}_N
  =
  -\uv_{\cdot j} + \bm{\varepsilon}_j,
  \label{eq:linear}
\end{equation}
where $\uv_{\cdot j}=(u_j^{(1)},\ldots,u_j^{(N)})^\top$ and
$\bm{\varepsilon}_j$ collects measurement noise and basis truncation error.

\subsection{Centering, scaling, and intercept recovery}
\label{sec:method:preprocess}

Because the basis columns generally have nonzero empirical means, the intercept
and the effect coefficients are estimated more stably after centering.  Let
$\bar{\Psib}=N^{-1}\mathbf{1}_N^\top\Psib$ and
$\bar u_j=N^{-1}\mathbf{1}_N^\top \uv_{\cdot j}$.  Define
\begin{equation}
  \Psic = \Psib-\mathbf{1}_N\bar{\Psib},
  \qquad
  \uv_{c,j} = \uv_{\cdot j}-\bar u_j\mathbf{1}_N.
  \label{eq:center}
\end{equation}
The centered regression is
\begin{equation}
  \Psic\thetav_j
  =
  -\uv_{c,j} + \bm{\varepsilon}_j.
  \label{eq:centered-linear}
\end{equation}
For numerical conditioning and fair penalization across basis columns, each
column of $\Psic$ is divided by its empirical standard deviation.  Let
$s_\ell=\operatorname{sd}(\bm{\Psi}_{c,\cdot\ell})$ and
$s_\ell\leftarrow \max(s_\ell,10^{-10})$ for protection against constant
columns.  With $S=\operatorname{diag}(s_1,\ldots,s_{pM})$, the scaled design is
$\Psic^{(s)}=\Psic S^{-1}$.  If $\hat{\thetav}_j^{(s)}$ is estimated on the
scaled design, the original-scale estimate is
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\thetav}_j = S^{-1}\hat{\thetav}_j^{(s)}.
  \label{eq:unscale}
\end{equation}
The baseline term is then recovered from the uncentered estimating equation:
\begin{equation}
  \hat\mu_j = -\bar u_j - \bar{\Psib}\hat{\thetav}_j.
  \label{eq:intercept}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Double-sparse estimation}
\label{sec:method:adsiht}

The coefficient vector has two natural sparsity structures.  First, the network
is sparse: for a fixed target node $j$, only a small number of source nodes have
nonzero cross-node effects.  Second, each active univariate effect is expected to
have a simple representation in the chosen dictionary.  Formally, we use
\begin{align}
  \left|\{i\neq j:\norm{\thetav_{ji}}_2>0\}\right| &\le s,
  \label{eq:sparse-group}\\
  \norm{\thetav_{ji}}_0 &\le s_0
  \qquad\text{for active } (j\leftarrow i).
  \label{eq:sparse-within}
\end{align}

Our primary estimator is adaptive double sparse iterative hard thresholding
(\adsiht{}; \citealp{zhang2023minimax}).  For each target node $j$, it solves
the scaled version of
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\thetav_j}
  \frac{1}{2N}
  \norm{\Psic\thetav_j+\uv_{c,j}}_2^2
  \quad
  \text{subject to \eqref{eq:sparse-group}--\eqref{eq:sparse-within}}.
  \label{eq:adsiht-objective}
\end{equation}
The grouping is defined by source node, with
\texttt{group = rep(1:p, each = M)}.  The information criterion in \adsiht{}
selects the effective sparsity levels adaptively, avoiding manual tuning of
$(s,s_0)$.

As a continuous-shrinkage comparator, we also consider the group lasso estimator
\citep{lounici2011group},
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\thetav_j}
  \left\{
  \frac{1}{2N}\norm{\Psic^{(s)}\thetav_j^{(s)}+\uv_{c,j}}_2^2
  + \lambda \sum_{i=1}^{p}\norm{\thetav_{ji}^{(s)}}_2
  \right\}.
  \label{eq:group-lasso}
\end{equation}
Unlike hard-thresholding, group lasso typically does not produce exact group
zeros, so edge selection requires a post-estimation threshold.

\subsection{Network reconstruction and local linear effects}
\label{sec:method:network}

The estimated edge set is obtained from the group norms of the fitted effects:
\begin{equation}
  \hat E
  =
  \{(j\leftarrow i): i\neq j,\ \norm{\hat{\thetav}_{ji}}_2\ge \tau_j\}.
  \label{eq:edge-estimate}
\end{equation}
For \adsiht{}, we use a floating-point threshold $\tau_j=10^{-10}$.  For group
lasso, we use the relative threshold
$\tau_j=0.01\max_{i\neq j}\norm{\hat{\thetav}_{ji}}_2$.

To summarize the local direction and magnitude of effects, we evaluate the
Jacobian of the fitted additive vector field at an unperturbed reference
steady state $\xv^{\mathrm{ref}}$:
\begin{equation}
  \hat J_{ji}
  =
  \left.
  \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}
  \left[
  \hat\mu_j + \hat f_{jj}(x_j)
  + \sum_{i'\neq j}\hat f_{ji'}(x_{i'})
  \right]
  \right|_{\xv=\xv^{\mathrm{ref}}}
  =
  \psiv'\!\left(x_i^{\mathrm{ref}}\right)^\top \hat{\thetav}_{ji}.
  \label{eq:jacobian}
\end{equation}
For the monomial dictionary, this reduces to
\begin{equation}
  \hat J_{ji}
  =
  \sum_{m=1}^{M}
  m\left(x_i^{\mathrm{ref}}\right)^{m-1}\hat\theta_{jim}.
  \label{eq:jacobian-monomial}
\end{equation}
A positive $\hat J_{ji}$ indicates that increasing node $i$ locally increases
the rate of change of node $j$ near the reference state, whereas a negative
value indicates a locally decreasing effect.

\subsection{Statistical interpretation}
\label{sec:method:theory}

For a fixed target node, the centered steady-state regression
\eqref{eq:centered-linear} is a high-dimensional grouped linear model with
structured sparsity.  Existing minimax theory for double-sparse regression
\citep{cai2022sparsegroup,zhang2023minimax} implies that, under suitable sparse
eigenvalue or restricted isometry conditions on $\Psic$, the squared estimation
error scales as
\begin{equation}
  \norm{\hat{\thetav}_j-\thetav_j^*}_2^2
  =
  O_{\mathbb{P}}\!\left[
  \frac{\sigma^2}{N}
  \left\{
  s\log\frac{p}{s}
  +
  ss_0\log\frac{eM}{s_0}
  \right\}
  \right].
  \label{eq:rate}
\end{equation}
The first term is the cost of locating the active source nodes, and the second
is the cost of selecting and estimating the active basis coefficients within
those nodes.  If the minimum active group norm is larger than the stochastic
estimation error, the same framework yields support recovery for the directed
edge set.  In the perturbed steady-state setting, these design conditions are
controlled by the choice of perturbations through the induced equilibrium
states; this motivates the perturbation-design experiments reported separately.
