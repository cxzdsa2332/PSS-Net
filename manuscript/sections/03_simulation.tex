\section{Simulation Studies}
\label{sec:simulation}

We evaluate PSS-Net around three questions: whether perturbed steady states
carry enough information to recover the directed sparse network and its
univariate edge functions; how recovery scales with sample budget and
perturbation design; and how PSS-Net compares with external
network-reconstruction baselines across topologies and measurement scales.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:sim:setup}

All experiments generate the full dynamical system but pass only the
post-perturbation equilibrium pairs $(\tilde{\xv}^{(k)},\uv^{(k)})$ to inference.
The main benchmark is the additive polynomial system
\begin{equation}
  \frac{dx_j}{dt}
  =
  \mu_j - \gamma_j x_j
  + \sum_{i\neq j}\{A_{ji}x_i + B_{ji}x_i^2\}
  + u_j,
  \label{eq:sim-additive}
\end{equation}
whose off-diagonal support defines the true directed network; $\gamma_j$ is set
large enough for diagonally stable, positive steady states, and quadratic terms
$B_{ji}$ are activated on a subset of edges in the nonlinear variants.  To
separate transient-model from steady-state-model specification we also use a
multiplicative generalized Lotka--Volterra (gLV) system
$\dot x_j = x_j(r_j + \sum_{i\neq j} A_{ji}x_i - \gamma_j x_j + u_j)$, whose
positive equilibrium reduces to the same additive linear steady-state equation
(\Cref{sec:method:pss}), so PSS-Net is correctly specified for the equilibrium
relation despite the multiplicative transient.

Unless stated otherwise, PSS-Net uses the node-wise \adsiht{} estimator
(\Cref{sec:method:adsiht}) with the no-intercept monomial library through second
order, i.e.\ source-node groups $\{x_i,x_i^2\}$.  Support recovery is summarized
by precision, recall, $F_1$, and Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC) on
off-diagonal directed edges, threshold-free ranking by AUPRC and AUROC, and
effect-strength recovery by coefficient/Jacobian error, sign accuracy, and
edge-function NRMSE.  All results are averaged over 30 random seeds and shown as
seed-level points with mean $\pm$ standard deviation.

\subsection{Foundation recovery}
\label{sec:sim:foundation}

The first group establishes what the estimator extracts from the data. We
visualize that only the final steady state---not the differentiated
trajectory---enters the regression, map the identifiability boundary at which
quadratic effects become detectable as a function of signal-to-noise ratio, and
compare \adsiht{} with group lasso under linear and nonlinear truth across
problem sizes. A fitted nonlinear system is then decomposed into baseline,
self-feedback, and received cross-node effects to confirm that selected edges
also reconstruct the correct function shape and sign, not merely an edge score.
A final library-misspecification experiment varies true (quadratic, saturating
Monod-type, sinusoidal) against fitted (linear, polynomial, Monod-type,
Fourier-type) dictionaries, separating support MCC---robust to moderate
misspecification---from edge-function NRMSE, which depends on whether the fitted
dictionary can represent the true shape.

\subsection{Scaling and perturbation design}
\label{sec:sim:design}

The second group studies how the same estimator behaves as the number and
placement of perturbations change. Sample complexity is organized by the
rescaled budget $N/(s\log p)$ across a range of network sizes and sparsity
levels. Perturbation designs then compare random, maximin, and D-optimal
strategies, the last mapping candidate perturbations through the (known or
locally linearized) steady-state response and selecting an exact subset with
\texttt{AlgDesign::optFederov()}
\citep{wheeler2025algdesign,fedorov1972theory}; the D-optimal backend is a
standard experimental-design tool, not a new inference algorithm. A two-stage
variant replaces the known response map with one estimated from a shared pilot,
comparing pilot-estimated D-optimal continuations against random, maximin, and
oracle D-optimal. The intended reading is regime-dependent: designed inputs help
most in nonlinear, low-budget settings, while at high budgets random
perturbations approach the same performance.

\subsection{External benchmarks, topology, and measurement limits}
\label{sec:sim:benchmark}

The third group places PSS-Net in a broader comparison. Against modular response
analysis \citep[MRA;][]{kholodenko2002untangling} via the aiMeRA implementation
\citep{jimenezdominguez2021aimera}, sparse linear regression (lasso and elastic
net through \texttt{glmnet}, \citealp{friedman2010glmnet}; group lasso,
\citealp{lounici2011group}), PySINDy-STLSQ
\citep{brunton2016sindy,kaptanoglu2022pysindy}, and association baselines
(Pearson correlation and partial correlation through \texttt{ppcor},
\citealp{kim2015ppcor}), PSS-Net matches the
best methods on linear truth and retains a margin in MCC and AUPRC under strong
nonlinearity, while uniquely providing absolute-scale Jacobian and
function-recovery diagnostics; row-normalized scores are used where output
scales differ. We test topology dependence by comparing node-wise and joint
multi-task PSS-Net on scale-free versus Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi graphs, reporting edge
MCC, out-degree rank correlation, and hub hit rate, to assess whether pooling
target equations helps recover hub-dominated structure. Finally, a compositional
experiment contrasts absolute abundance (an oracle upper bound) with closed
relative abundance, centered log-ratio, and noisy-total-corrected scales, to
quantify the loss from closure and whether an external biomass measurement
suffices to recover the absolute-scale steady-state equation.
